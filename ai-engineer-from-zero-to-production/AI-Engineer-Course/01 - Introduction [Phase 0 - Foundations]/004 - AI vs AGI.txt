# AI vs AGI

## Overview
This lesson clarifies the distinction between today's Artificial Intelligence — narrow, task-specific systems — and Artificial General Intelligence (AGI), which remains theoretical. Understanding this boundary is essential for AI Engineers to set realistic expectations with stakeholders, avoid over-promising, and build systems that are grounded in what current models can actually do reliably.

## Key Points
- Narrow AI (what we have today) refers to systems that excel at one category of task — image classification, text generation, code completion — but cannot transfer that competence to fundamentally different domains without retraining or re-prompting.
- AGI refers to a hypothetical AI system with human-level general reasoning ability: it could learn any intellectual task a human can, transfer knowledge across domains spontaneously, and operate with true autonomy — no such system exists as of 2025.
- Large Language Models (GPT-4, Claude 3.5, Gemini 1.5) are very capable narrow AI systems — they exhibit impressive emergent behaviors (chain-of-thought reasoning, few-shot learning) but still fail in predictable ways: hallucination, poor arithmetic, limited causal reasoning, and sensitivity to prompt phrasing.
- The practical implication for AI Engineers: always treat LLM outputs as probabilistic, not deterministic — you must build evaluation, validation, and fallback layers rather than trusting the model blindly.
- Hype cycles around AGI timelines (e.g., "AGI by 2027" claims) directly affect product teams and funding — AI Engineers must stay grounded in empirical benchmarks (MMLU, HumanEval, GPQA) rather than media narratives.

## Code Example
```python
# Demonstrating narrow AI limitations — practical examples AI Engineers encounter

from openai import OpenAI

client = OpenAI()


def test_llm_capability(task: str, prompt: str) -> str:
    """Run a task and return the model's response."""
    response = client.chat.completions.create(
        model="gpt-4o-mini",
        messages=[{"role": "user", "content": prompt}],
        temperature=0,
    )
    result = response.choices[0].message.content
    print(f"\n[Task: {task}]")
    print(f"Prompt: {prompt}")
    print(f"Response: {result}")
    return result


if __name__ == "__main__":
    # Example 1: LLMs are excellent at language tasks (narrow AI strength)
    test_llm_capability(
        task="Text summarization",
        prompt="Summarize in one sentence: The transformer architecture, introduced in 2017, "
               "revolutionized NLP by replacing recurrence with self-attention mechanisms.",
    )

    # Example 2: LLMs struggle with reliable arithmetic (narrow AI limitation)
    test_llm_capability(
        task="Arithmetic (known failure mode)",
        prompt="What is 17 multiplied by 83, minus 214, divided by 7? Show no working.",
    )
    # Note: For reliable math, route to a code interpreter or calculator tool,
    # not the LLM's own arithmetic — this is an AI Engineer's job.

    # Example 3: LLMs hallucinate facts (a key production risk)
    test_llm_capability(
        task="Fact recall (hallucination risk)",
        prompt="What was the exact closing price of Apple stock on March 15, 2023?",
    )
    # An AGI would know its own knowledge boundaries and refuse confidently.
    # Narrow AI systems often confabulate plausible-sounding but wrong answers.

    # Production pattern: always validate factual claims with retrieval (RAG)
    print("\n[Production recommendation]")
    print("For factual queries, use RAG to ground answers in verified sources.")
    print("Never rely on LLM parametric memory alone for business-critical facts.")
```

## Notes
The AGI debate can be a distraction for working engineers — what matters practically is the capability-reliability frontier of today's models. A system that is 95% accurate on average can still be catastrophic in production if the 5% failures are silent and undetected. The most important AGI-related concept for AI Engineers is "alignment": current models do not have goals of their own, but they can be manipulated (prompt injection) or fail in unexpected ways at the edges of their training distribution. Watch OpenAI's model cards, Anthropic's model safety reports, and independent benchmarks like HELM or BIG-bench for current capability assessments — do not rely on vendor marketing.

## Summary
Today's AI systems are powerful but narrow — they excel within their training distribution and fail in predictable ways outside it — and as an AI Engineer your job is to design systems that are safe, reliable, and honest about those limitations rather than pretending AGI already exists.
